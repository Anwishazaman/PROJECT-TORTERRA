Willow Oak

Provides great color, with leaves starting out bright green in the spring, shifting to a deeper summertime green, and then turning to fall shades of yellow bronze-orange, yellow-brown, and russet-red Features spear-shaped leaves that are 2-5" long, each with a tiny bristle at the tip.Grows in a pyramidal shape in its youth then an oblong-oval to rounded shape at maturity Tolerates poorly drained soil

River Birch

Develops a cinnamon-colored bark that curls and peels (once mature) Can grow as either a single- or multi-stemmed tree Features glossy green leaves that are 2-3" long and somewhat triangular; margins are double-toothed and leaves are arranged alternately Produces brown and green catkins in April and May.Should not be planted in very alkaline soil

Red Maple

Provides amazing fall color that is yellow to red Produces red (sometimes yellow) clusters of small flowers winter to spring Features simple, medium to dark green leaves 2-6" in length with 3 or 5 lobes and sinuses that are irregularly toothed.Can grow in an oval, rounded, upright or erect shape Can be toxic to horses if dry, wilted leaves are consumed

Quaking Aspen

Adds movement and a soft, pleasant sound to the landscape due to the "quaking" leaves Has smooth, greenish-white to cream bark on a long, narrow trunk Features rounded, triangular leaves with small teeth on the margins and arranged alternately on the branches Produces long, silvery catkins typically in April and MayIs one of the first trees to spring up after a forest fire Sends up sprouts from its shallow, wide-spreading roots.

Black Walnut

Yields a ripened nut crop in early to mid-autumn that consists of three layers: a green, fleshy husk; a black inner shell that is hard, thick and corrugated; and the kernel, which is oily and sweet Begins to bear nuts in 12-15 years.Is self-fertile but requires wind for pollination; plant more than one tree to ensure a better crop Grows in a rounded shape Develops a deep taproot, making it difficult to transplant

Green Ash

Fraxinus pennsylvanica (Green Ash) is a medium-sized deciduous tree reaching 12-25 metres (rarely to 45 m) tall with a trunk up to 60 centimetres in diameter. The bark is smooth and gray on young trees, becoming thick. It is the most widely distributed of all the American ashes, although its range centers on the midwestern U.S. and Great Plains. The natural habitat of green ash is almost exclusively stream sides and bottomlands.

Cottonwood

Populus deltoides (Eastern Cottonwood) is 20-60 m tall and with a trunk up to 2.8 m diameter, one of the largest North American hardwood trees. The bark is silvery-white, lightly fissured when young, deeply fissured and dark grey on old trees. In Mississippi, height growth of 3-5 m per year for a few years has been seen. Sustained height growth of 1.5 m height growth and 2.5 cm diameter growth per year for 25 years is common.

Prairie Fire Crabapple

Blooms in a profusion of dark pink to red blossoms in April and May Is disease-resistant Features simple oval leaves 1-4" in length; coloring starts a glossy maroon or purplish-red in spring, turning dark green with purplish-red veins in the summer then a beautiful bronze color in autumn. Is self-fertile, meaning it depends on insects such as bees to transfer pollen between flowers on the same tree. Grows in a rounded, oval shape
